Profit and Loss

Profit and loss are fundamental financial terms representing your net earnings or net losses after all costs are deducted from your revenue over a specific period. When your revenues exceed expenses, you record a profit, indicating successful financial performance. Conversely, if expenses surpass revenues, the outcome is a loss, signaling a financial deficit. Maintaining a detailed profit and loss statement (P&L statement) is essential for analyzing financial health, guiding decision-making, and ensuring sustainable business operations or personal budgeting.

In investment contexts like the stock market and mutual funds, profit and loss indicate the financial outcome of buying and selling assets based on their changing market values.

Stock Market

Profit transpires when you sell shares at a price higher than your purchase price. For example, buying shares for ₹100 each and selling them at ₹150 generates a ₹50 gain per share. Loss arises when shares are sold below the purchase price; for instance, buying at ₹100 and selling at ₹80 results in a ₹20 loss per share. The net gain or loss calculation must consider brokerage fees and transaction costs:

Profit or Loss = Selling Price − Purchase Price − Transaction Costs

Mutual Funds

Mutual funds aggregate capital from multiple investors to purchase diversified portfolios of stocks, bonds, or other securities. The fund’s value is reflected by its Net Asset Value (NAV) per unit. Profit, also known as capital gain, occurs when the selling NAV exceeds the purchase NAV. Additionally, investors may receive dividends, representing a portion of the fund's earnings distributed to unit holders. Loss occurs if the NAV declines below the purchase price upon selling. The formula for profit or loss per unit is:

Profit or Loss per unit = Selling NAV − Purchase NAV

Investors should be aware that neither stock prices nor mutual fund NAVs are guaranteed to increase; they are subject to market risks and fluctuations.
