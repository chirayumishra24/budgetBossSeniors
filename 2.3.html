My Notes

What is Investment?

Investment involves using your saved money to buy assets like stocks, bonds, or mutual funds, aiming to grow your wealth over time. Unlike savings, investments carry risks but offer the potential for higher returns. Understanding investment basics helps you make informed choices.

Key Insight: Investments involve risk but can lead to greater wealth if chosen wisely.

Types of Investments

Type of Investment Description Examples
Stocks (Equity) Buying ownership shares in a company; potential for dividends and capital gains. High risk, high return. Individual company stocks (e.g., Reliance, TCS)
Bonds (Fixed Income) Lending money to governments or corporations, earning fixed interest. Lower risk than stocks. Government bonds, corporate bonds
Mutual Funds Pooled investments managed by professionals, investing in stocks, bonds, or a mix. Diversifies risk. Equity funds, debt funds, hybrid funds
Real Estate Investing in property for rental income or capital appreciation. Illiquid and long-term. Residential or commercial properties
Cash and Cash Equivalents Safe, liquid investments with low returns, used for short-term needs. Savings accounts, fixed deposits
Alternative Investments Non-traditional assets like private equity, hedge funds, or cryptocurrencies. Gold, cryptocurrency

The Right Type of Investment for a High School Student

When you’re in high school, you may not have a regular income yet, but you can start learning how to make your money grow wisely. The goal at this stage is to build financial habits and understand how investments work, not to take big risks.

1. Savings Account with Interest

The simplest and safest starting point.

  • You can open a student savings account (often with zero balance or low fees).
  • The bank pays interest — meaning your money grows a little every month.
Good for: Learning the habit of saving regularly and tracking growth.

2. Recurring Deposit (RD)

You deposit a fixed amount every month (e.g., ₹500 or ₹1,000).

  • The bank gives a slightly higher interest rate than a regular savings account.
  • At the end of the period (say 1 year), you get your money plus the interest earned.
Good for: Building discipline and understanding how time increases value.

3. Investing in Knowledge or Skills

The best investment for any student!

  • Spend on books, online courses, workshops, or tools that develop your skills (coding, music, design, art, etc.).
  • These skills can help you earn money or get better opportunities later.
Good for: Long-term personal growth and future income.

4. Mutual Funds (with Guidance)

If you are open, you can learn about Systematic Investment Plans (SIPs) — investing small amounts regularly in mutual funds.

  • This helps you understand how the stock market works — without needing a lot of money.
Good for: Learning the basics of financial growth and compounding.
